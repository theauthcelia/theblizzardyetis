Avalanche Information

WHAT IS AN AVALANCHE?

An avalanche is a sudden flow of snow down a slope, often triggered by weather, terrain, or human activity. They can move fast and be extremely dangerous. Avalanches are powerful enough to bury people, destroy trees, and sweep away anything in their path. Snow conditions, temperature changes, and wind can all increase the risk.

How to Stay Safe ?

  • Check Snow Conditions– Snowpack stability changes daily, so always assess the terrain before traveling.
  • Be Weather Aware – Heavy snowfall, wind, and temperature shifts can increase avalanche risk.
  • Use Safety Gear – Essential equipment includes a beacon, probe, and shovel for rescue situations.
  • Know When to Turn Back – If conditions seem unsafe, it’s better to change plans than take a risk.

WHAT TO DO IF I GET CAUGHT ?

1.Stay on top: Try to “swim” to stay near the surface. (Like a snow angel, but cooler.)

2.Protect airway: Use one arm to keep that air pocket. (Gotta breathe, folks!)

3.Stay calm: Slow down that breathing and chill out. (Literally, don’t panic.)

4.Keep head up: You want that face above the snow. (No snowball fights with your face!)

5.Use your beacon: Flick it on, let rescuers find you. (Signal like you're the star of the show!)

6.Signal for help: Wave ‘em down. (Get that attention!)

WHAT TO DO IF BURIED?

1.Stay Calm & Create an Air Pocket – Use your hands to make space in front of your face to help you breathe.

2.Try to Move – If possible, shift your body to create space before the snow settles.

3.Spit to Find Up – If you’re disoriented, spit and see which way it falls—gravity will show you which way is down.

4.Call for Help – If you have a radio or beacon, activate it. Yell only if you hear rescuers nearby to conserve air.

5.Stay Still & Conserve Oxygen – Panic burns oxygen. If you can’t escape, stay calm and wait for rescue.

EMERGENCY CONTACT AND RESOURCES

In case of an avalanche or any other emergency in the backcountry, it's crucial to act quickly. Always have access to the following emergency contacts and resources before venturing out.

For immediate assistance, call Avalanche Rescue Teams at 1-800-123-4567 or your local emergency services.

In the case of hypothermia, symptoms include shivering, confusion, and slurred speech—seek shelter, warm the body gradually, and contact emergency services immediately. For emergency assistance, you can reach the Avalanche Rescue Team at 1-800-123-4567 or Mountain Rescue Services at 1-800-234-5678.

For additional support, visit www.rescue.org or more resources on avalanche safety and mountain emergency preparedness.
